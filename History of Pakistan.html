History of Pakistan

From Indus Valley to Moder world

Pre-Partation

Indus Valley Civalization:




The Indus Valley Civilization (IVC), also known as the Harappan Civilization, flourished around 2500 BCE in the northwestern regions of South Asia, primarily in present-day Pakistan and northwest India. It is one of the world's oldest and most advanced ancient civilizations, contemporaneous with ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia. The Indus Valley Civilization extended across a vast area of over 1.25 million square kilometers, encompassing parts of modern-day Pakistan, India, and Afghanistan. Key urban centers included Harappa and Mohenjo-Daro in Pakistan, and Dholavira and Lothal in India. These cities were well-planned with sophisticated infrastructure, including grid-like street layouts, advanced drainage systems, and multi-storied residential buildings.

The Mughal Empire, spanning from the early 16th to the mid-18th century, represents a golden age in Indian history marked by remarkable cultural and architectural achievements. Central to their legacy is the Taj Mahal, perhaps the most iconic symbol of Mughal architecture and a UNESCO World Heritage site.

Commissioned by Emperor Shah Jahan in the 17th century as a mausoleum for his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal, the Taj Mahal stands as a testament to love and craftsmanship. Its intricate marble façade adorned with delicate carvings and semi-precious stones, coupled with its perfectly symmetrical gardens and reflecting pool, exemplifies the pinnacle of Indo-Islamic architecture. The Taj Mahal's harmonious blend of Persian, Turkish, Indian, and Islamic architectural styles reflects the Mughal Empire's cultural synthesis and aesthetic refinement.

Pakistan Movement

Allama Iqbal, a philosopher, poet, and politician, significantly contributed to the ideological foundation of Pakistan. His poetry and philosophical works emphasized the spiritual and cultural unity of Muslims and the need for a separate homeland where they could freely practice their religion

and preserve their cultural identity. In his famous Allahabad Address of 1930, Iqbal articulated the vision of an independent Muslim state in north-western India, which later inspired the demand for Pakistan. He envisioned a society based on Islamic principles of justice, equality, and solidarity. While Iqbal did not live to see the creation of Pakistan, his ideas profoundly influenced Jinnah and the Muslim League, providing a moral and philosophical justification for the demand for a separate state. Together, Jinnah's political leadership and Iqbal's ideological contributions galvanized the Muslim community, leading to the eventual partition of India and the establishment of Pakistan.




The cities of the Indus Valley were remarkable for their uniformity and advanced urban planning. Streets were laid out in perfect grid patterns, and buildings were constructed using standardized fired and sun-dried bricks. Public buildings, such as the Great Bath of Mohenjo-Daro, indicate a highly organized social structure with emphasis on cleanliness and public health. The presence of granaries suggests advanced agricultural practices and surplus food storage capabilities. The economy of the Indus Valley Civilization was predominantly agricultural, with wheat, barley, and peas being the main crops. Trade played a crucial role in the civilization’s prosperity, with evidence of extensive trade networks reaching Mesopotamia, Central Asia, and possibly even Egypt. Artifacts such as seals, beads, and pottery indicate a thriving craft industry. The discovery of weights and measures suggests a standardized system of trade and commerce.

Mughal Empire













Beyond the Taj Mahal, the Mughals left a legacy of forts, palaces, mosques, and gardens that exemplify their architectural prowess. The Agra Fort, also commissioned by Emperor Akbar and later renovated by Shah Jahan, stands as a colossal red sandstone citadel showcasing a fusion of Islamic, Persian, and Hindu architectural elements. In Delhi, the Red Fort, another architectural marvel, served as the main residence of the emperors for nearly 200 years. Its impressive gateways, marble palaces, and sprawling gardens highlight the grandeur and strategic planning characteristic of Mughal constructions. Moreover, the city of Fatehpur Sikri, built by Akbar and later abandoned, remains a UNESCO World Heritage Site famous for its palaces, courtyards, and the Buland Darwaza, a triumphal gateway considered one of the tallest in the world. In essence, the Mughal Empire's architectural achievements not only defined an era of artistic expression but also continue to inspire awe and admiration across the globe.

The pre-partition history of India is marked by a complex interplay of political, social, and cultural dynamics that eventually led to the creation of Pakistan in 1947. During the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the Indian subcontinent was under British colonial rule, which prompted various movements for independence. Among the prominent leaders advocating for an independent Muslim state were Muhammad Ali Jinnah and Allama Iqbal. Jinnah, initially a member of the

Indian National Congress, later became the leader of the All-India Muslim League. His journey from advocating Hindu-Muslim unity to demanding a separate nation for Muslims was influenced by his belief that Muslims in India needed a distinct political identity to protect their rights and interests. Jinnah's legal acumen, political strategy, and oratory skills were instrumental in articulating the demands of Muslims and negotiating with the British government, culminating in the Lahore Resolution of 1940, which called for separate states for Muslims in north-western and eastern zones of India.

Post partation:

After the partition of British India in 1947, Pakistan emerged as an independent nation, marking a significant milestone in South Asian history. This momentous event was not just the birth of a new country but also the culmination of a long struggle for self-determination by the Muslims of the Indian subcontinent. The newly formed nation of Pakistan inherited a plethora of challenges that tested the resilience and ingenuity of its leaders and citizens. One of the most pressing challenges was the need to establish a functional government from scratch. Unlike India, which inherited the more developed infrastructure of British colonial rule, Pakistan had to build its administrative and governmental frameworks almost entirely anew. This task was compounded by the fact that the two wings of Pakistan—East Pakistan (now Bangladesh) and West Pakistan—were separated by over a thousand miles of Indian territory, creating logistical and administrative complexities.

Mohammad Ali Jinnah, the founding father of Pakistan, became the country's first Governor-General, a role in which his leadership proved indispensable during the critical initial years of the new nation. Jinnah, revered as "Quaid-e-Azam" (Great Leader), tirelessly worked to lay the foundation of a state that he envisioned as a homeland where all citizens, irrespective of their religion or ethnicity, could live in harmony and enjoy equal rights. His vision was not merely political but deeply humanitarian, advocating for a nation built on the principles of justice, tolerance, and inclusion. Jinnah's speeches and policies consistently emphasized the importance of unity and faith, aiming to forge a national identity that transcended the communal divides which had plagued the subcontinent. After Jinnah's untimely death in 1948, Liaquat Ali Khan, his close associate and confidant, assumed the role of Prime Minister. Liaquat Ali Khan's leadership was crucial in this transformative period as he endeavored to consolidate the nascent state's institutions and steer it through its formative challenges. He was instrumental in shaping Pakistan's early foreign policy, seeking to establish the country as a sovereign, independent state in the international community.

Over the subsequent decades, Pakistan saw a range of leaders who contributed to its development and navigated the country through periods of both progress and turmoil.
